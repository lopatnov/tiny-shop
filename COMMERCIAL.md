## What counts as commercial use (a paid license is required)

- Using the software or its source code in a product or service that generates revenue.
- Running it as a paid or monetized marketplace / SaaS.
- Embedding the code (in whole or in part) into a commercial product.
- Any for-profit activity that is not a "permitted purpose" under the `LICENSE`.

Noncommercial use (personal, educational, research, or by noncommercial organizations) is free
under the `LICENSE`; no separate license is needed.

## Draft commercial terms (negotiable; final version via a lawyer)

1. **Royalty — a percentage of REVENUE.** The licensee pays `<X>`% of the gross revenue
   attributable to the product or service that uses this software or its code.
   - Tied to revenue, NOT net profit (profit is easy to understate).
   - The exact rate is set in negotiation; typically a fraction of a percent to a few percent
     of revenue.
2. **Audit right.** The licensor may periodically review revenue reporting to verify royalties.
3. **Paid improvements.** If the licensor makes improvements on a paid basis, **the rights to
   those improvements remain with the licensor**, who may include them in the product and
   **resell / license them to others**. (Exclusivity for a customer is a separate, pricier
   option.)
4. **Copyright retained.** Copyright in the software stays with the licensor; a commercial
   license grants usage rights on agreed terms — it is not an assignment of rights.
5. **Optional minimum fee.** A minimum payment / period on top of the royalty can be added,
   negotiable per customer.

## Third-party contributions (for the future)

If external contributors appear, introduce a **CLA (Contributor License Agreement)** so their
contributions do not limit the licensor's right to commercialize and relicense the project.
